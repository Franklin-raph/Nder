UX Techniques

Appstah is an Australian-based software engineering company whose mission is to produce incredibly high quality and capable mobile applications for individuals new to Tech, Startups, Businesses, Government, and anyone with a tangible idea.

The Problem

While Appstah has successfully created a system to provide this service, our challenge is the need to promote/market our services to our prospective clients while making it fairly easy for our clients all over the world to send us a message and check out our services with how we would play an important role in their wonderful businesses or ideas.

The primary business needs from the brief were:

  1. We want a front where new clients can come and have a full idea of all our services and how we can help their businesses.
  2. and then send us a direct message.

The Solution

We designed the Appstah website to provide users with detailed information about their mobile app services and a means to send a direct message to the company with details about their business or ideas and budget. This would help establish a ground for the company to show mastery in their area of operations, increase awareness of their services, create a ground for negotiation, and ultimately make it easy for their users to send a direct message to the company.

Competitor/Comparative Analysis

I conducted a competitor analysis to gain inspiration and identify best practices for our website design. I began looking at the websites of several other software engineering companies in Australia and then the world including https://dreamwalk.com.au/, https://www.intellectsoft.net/, https://litslink.com/, and https://www.arcadedevhouse.com.au/

Discovery

One common feature I identified across these websites was the placement of the “Contact Button” in order to emphasize it as the main call to action. This was an important design pattern to consider in order to make it easier and more efficient for users to send a direct message or contact the company via other means like the such of Facebook, LinkedIn, WhatsApp, Instagram, and Twitter.

Another common design pattern I observed was that each software company prominently displayed its mission statement at the beginning of its homepage. This was an important method to effectively communicate each software company’s goal to the user and help them better understand what each company does. Similarly, I observed another design pattern that software companies had a direct message or contact us designed at the end of their websites (homepage, services page, and about us page). This was done to help the user send a direct message without the hassle of going to the contact page after being convinced about the company. On the services page, I noticed many software companies had a sketch or illustration to show a well-detailed software development process in order to make the user understand the work and process put into the development of a mobile app.

Information Architecture and User Flow

Once I organized all my research and defined our solution, I created a user flow, which is the user journey from start to finish without excess details. Then I created the initial Information Architecture which I changed along the way. I did this to have a direct goal in mind and not to be distracted when coming up with potential designs.

Wireframe

There wasn’t enough time for multiple sketches, so after sketching a single idea I went into figma and made a digital sketch. I used the digital sketch for an interview with the CEO who just wanted to include a mapto the homepage which I added before going to high fidelity design.
